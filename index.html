😊 Real vs. fake smiles (the Duchenne test)›

A genuine smile fires the orbicularis oculi — the muscle ringing the eye — producing crow's-feet wrinkles and lifted cheeks. A social or forced smile moves the mouth only.

  • Genuine: eyes narrow, cheeks rise, crow's feet appear.
  • Fake: mouth smiles, eyes stay flat; often switches on/off too abruptly and lasts too long.

CueSight's "Smile type" cue runs this check live using the cheek-squint muscles.

⚖️ What science actually says about lie detection›

The landmark Bond & DePaulo meta-analysis (206 studies, 24,000+ judges) found people detect lies at 54% accuracy — barely above a coin flip. Police and judges score no better than students.

Myths debunked

  • "Looking up-and-to-the-right = lying" (NLP) — rejected by 23 of 24 peer-reviewed studies.
  • Gaze aversion, fidgeting, face-touching as lie signs — no reliable link.
  • Micro-expressions as lie proof — they reveal a concealed emotion, not why. A truthful person under suspicion feels fear too (the "Othello error").

What helps evidence-based

  • Baseline first: learn how this person acts when relaxed; watch for clusters of deviation, never single cues.
  • Cognitive load: lying is mentally harder. Unexpected questions and telling a story in reverse order degrade fabricated accounts more than true ones.
  • Content over body: real memories carry more sensory detail and complications; fabricated stories are smoother, shorter and stay inconsistent across retellings.
  • Weak real correlates: fewer details, vocal tension/raised pitch, lip pressing, fewer genuine smiles, fewer illustrative hand gestures, dilated pupils.
🔬 The fear-leak research CueSight uses (Shen et al., 2021)›

A Frontiers in Psychology study analyzed real high-stakes game-show footage with computer vision, using only the facial muscle movements of fear (Action Units 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 20, 26). Question Mode now implements its three findings:

  • Fear leaks discriminate: machine-learning classifiers reached 85–90% accuracy on high-stakes clips from fear AUs alone.
  • AU20 (horizontal lip stretch) is the hot spot: when lying it was weaker and more fleeting — median 267 ms vs 400 ms when truthful — because liars actively choke it off. CueSight times every AU20 burst and scores "fleetingness."
  • Left/right asymmetry: facial movements desynchronize when lying (coherence 0.71 vs 0.81). CueSight correlates your left and right brow signals across each answer.

Caveats the authors themselves give: 16 participants, high-stakes TV context, and leaked fear is a hot spot — not proof of lying. Truth-tellers under pressure feel fear too. That's why the 🎯 calibration matters: it learns how this person looks when truthful vs lying, instead of comparing them to a TV game show.

🫥 Body language beyond the face›
  • Happy / confident: open posture, uncrossed arms, leaning in, animated gestures.
  • Sad / low: downward gaze, slumped shoulders, minimal facial movement, slow speech.
  • Anxious: leg bouncing, lip biting, locked ankles, rapid blinking, self-soothing (neck, hair, hands).
  • Defensive: crossed arms, body angled away, feet toward the exit, objects held as barriers.
  • Engaged: sustained eye contact, mirroring your posture, head tilts, nodding.
  • Pupils can't be consciously controlled: they dilate with interest, arousal and mental effort.
🔒 Ethics, consent & legal›
  • Get consent before recording anyone. Many US states require all-party consent for audio recording of conversations — using the mic on someone unaware may be illegal where you live.
  • All processing happens on your device — CueSight sends no video or audio anywhere.
  • Never use cue readings to accuse someone. Use them to decide where to ask better questions.
